# Uber 2022 Security Breach Case Study

## Overview

This repository contains a comprehensive cybersecurity case study analyzing the **2022 Uber security breach**, one of the most well-known examples of how social engineering and identity-based attacks can bypass traditional security controls. The project consisted of both a **written analytical report** and an **oral presentation** that examined the technical and human factors that contributed to the breach, evaluated Uber's incident response, and proposed recommendations to prevent similar attacks.

As part of this project, our team presented our findings to an audience of approximately **50 people**, using PowerPoint slides to communicate the attack timeline, vulnerabilities, response strategy, and lessons learned.

---

## Project Objectives

The primary objectives of this case study were to:

- Analyze the events surrounding the 2022 Uber security breach.
- Identify the technical, organizational, and human factors that enabled the attack.
- Evaluate Uber's security controls and determine why they failed.
- Develop an Incident Response Plan (IRP) following industry best practices.
- Recommend preventative security measures to reduce the likelihood and impact of future attacks.
- Strengthen technical research, written communication, teamwork, and public speaking skills through professional reporting and presentation.

## My Contributions

### Written Report

I authored the **Prevention Recommendations** section of the report, focusing on security controls that could have prevented or significantly reduced the impact of the breach.

My analysis covered:

- Security awareness and employee training to defend against social engineering
- Implementing Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) for earlier threat detection
- Eliminating hardcoded credentials through secure programming practices
- Improving organizational security policies and defensive strategies

This section emphasized practical cybersecurity controls that organizations can implement to defend against identity-based attacks and privilege escalation.

### Presentation

For the accompanying oral presentation, I designed and presented the slide covering **Social Engineering**, explaining:

- How attackers manipulated human behavior rather than exploiting software vulnerabilities
- MFA fatigue attacks
- IT impersonation techniques
- Why human error remains one of the largest cybersecurity risks
- Lessons organizations can learn to improve user awareness and authentication practices

The presentation was delivered to an audience of approximately **50 attendees**, using the slides to communicate complex cybersecurity concepts in an engaging and accessible manner.
